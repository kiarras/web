Vertical electrical soundings

One of the parameters whose knowledge can help to differentiate lithologies in the subsoil or to identify foreign bodies in a more or less uniform environment is the electrical resistivity

Obtaining the distribution of this parameter is one of the fundamental objectives of Geophysical Prospecting

There are numerous devices and methodologies to try to meet this objective

When the purpose is to find out the vertical distribution of resistivities, and is done by measurements in direct current, we are before a Vertical Electrical Survey (VES).

In general terms, it is a question of creating an electric field by injecting DC into the ground to be studied through two electrodes (usually called current electrodes A and B), and studying this field with the use of two other electrodes ( Called potential electrodes M and N). The resulting electric field will depend, among other variables, on the distribution of resistivities of the subsurface structures, the distances between the electrodes and their relative arrangement and the injected current.

Among the different combinations of the relative positions of the four electrodes mentioned, the most usual is that of the so-called SEV Schlumberger. Schematically responds to the following configuration:

Where the electrodes are aligned, the distance AM is equal to the NB and the distance MN must be very small compared to the AB (in the calculations it is assimilated to a point). The successive measurements are obtained by gradually separating the current electrodes A and B; The greater this distance the greater the depth of investigation.

The higher efficiency of the method corresponds to the case in which the SEVs are carried out on a ground composed of laterally homogeneous layers with respect to resistivity and bounded by planes parallel to the surface of the terrain (stratified medium). Experience has shown that the theoretical results obtained for media of this kind are tolerably valid for strata inclined up to about 30 Â°. For greater inclinations or lateral changes, it is preferable to use Electrical Tomography.

Applications

HYDROGEOLOGY: Localization of geological structures that can function as aquifers, such as coarse-grained detrital (medium and high resistivities), karstified limestones (high and very high resistivities), etc.
GEOTECNIA: Definition of geological structures differentiable by their resistivity. As for example alluvial, marls under limestones (structure not detectable with refraction seismic due to the inversion of speeds), delimitation of areas of "loans", etc. Resistivity measurements for Earth Taps.
MINING: Detection of mineral masses that respond by their resistivity contrast. Quarry study.
ARCHEOLOGY: Detection of structures with sufficient resistivity contrast with their surroundings.
